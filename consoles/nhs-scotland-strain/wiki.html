1 · What this is — and why NHS, not VA

Is: a hospital-system strain console for NHS Scotland, running on real, published A&E, bed-occupancy and delayed-discharge data — the same early-warning engine as the St. Petersburg water and VA consoles, but on live public data.
Is NOT: a live bedside monitor, a hospital league table for blame, or a prediction of future performance.

The companion VA strain console had to ship with demo data because U.S. VA real-time strain (bed census, ED boarding, staffing) is internal and not public. The question "is NHS data more available?" has a clear answer: yes. NHS England publishes operational strain by named trust; NHS Scotland goes further and offers a genuine open API. This console is built on that — so the figures are real, current, and traceable to source.

2 · The data & its cadence

All operational data comes from opendata.nhs.scot, the Public Health Scotland CKAN open-data portal. Four datasets feed the console:

Why a snapshot, not a live fetch? The CKAN API is keyless and returns JSON, but it sends no CORS header, so a browser opening a local file is blocked from calling it directly. The console therefore embeds a dated real snapshot (the same pattern the markets console uses for equities) and documents how to refresh it (Section 11). Given the data only updates weekly at best, a dated snapshot loses almost nothing and gains reliability and offline use.

3 · System strain index MEASURED

A single 0–100 read of unscheduled-care pressure, built only from the real figures on the page — no hidden model, no fitted weights to past crises:

  • A&E gap (40%) — how far national Type-1 4-hour performance sits below the 95% standard.
  • Long-wait share (20%) — patients over 12 hours as a share of attendances.
  • Bed occupancy (15%) — mean board occupancy above the safe-flow threshold.
  • Delayed discharge (25%) — the census backlog of patients who can't leave.

Bands: Normal (<40), Elevated (40–57), High pressure (58–73), Severe (≥74). On the real baseline it reads High pressure (~60) — an honest reflection that Scottish unscheduled care has run well below standard for the whole period shown. The component chips show each driver, so you can see why the index is where it is.

4 · A&E 4-hour performance MEASURED

The headline NHS pressure metric: the share of attendances at major (Type 1) emergency departments dealt with within four hours. The Scottish Government standard is 95% (the blue line). The trend shows the last 30 weeks; the orange line sitting persistently around 60–65% — far below the blue line and barely moving — is the clearest single picture of chronic rather than acute strain.

5 · ED strain ranking MEASURED

The trust-ranking equivalent: every major (Type 1) ED, worst first by 4-hour performance for the latest week, with the 95% standard marked by a blue tick and the count of patients who waited over 12 hours alongside. This identifies where the pressure concentrates. Treat it as a focus tool, not a judgment: small/rural EDs and large urban majors face very different case-mix, and a single week is noisy — read it with the national trend.

6 · The acute-harm signal — long waits MEASURED

Averages hide harm. This panel surfaces the count of patients waiting over 8 and over 12 hours in major EDs. Long ED waits are independently associated with increased 30-day mortality (the evidence base behind the move to report 12-hour waits at all), so this — not the 4-hour average — is the number to watch when judging patient safety.

7 · Bed occupancy MEASURED · 2025 Q4

Average occupancy of staffed acute beds, by health board. Rules of thumb: above ~85% (amber) patient flow slows and waits grow; above ~92% (red) infection control and safety degrade sharply. Scotland's system-wide mean sits lower than England's, so occupancy is a moderate contributor here — which is itself an honest finding, not a downplaying.

8 · Delayed discharges — the real cause MEASURED

This is the upstream driver of everything above. At the monthly census, these are patients clinically ready to leave hospital but unable to — the "no criteria to reside" backlog, usually waiting on social care or a care-home place. They occupy beds that admitted ED patients need, which is why a full ward and a gridlocked A&E are usually a social-care problem wearing a hospital costume. Shown by board, worst first, with the Scotland total.

9 · Scenario sandbox

Pressure-test the response plan against the real baseline. Four what-ifs adjust the inputs and recompute the strain index: winter surge (4-hour −15pp, occupancy +5, delays +15%), norovirus bed closures, staffing shortfall, discharge gridlock (delays +40%). These are clearly labelled as what-ifs, never forecasts — they exist to rehearse decisions, e.g. "a winter surge tips us from High pressure to Severe; is the escalation plan ready for that?"

10 · England comparator CITED — context, not live

For context, England runs the same 4-hour standard (95%), with an interim operational target of 78%. The latest reported figures (May 2026): 75.7% of all-types A&E attendances within 4 hours, and roughly 50,000 patients waiting over 12 hours from decision-to-admit that month (King's Fund / NHS England). England has no equivalent open-data API, so these are cited, not shown live.

Do not read this as a league table. England's 75.7% is all-types; the console's Scottish figure (~64%) is major (Type-1) EDs only, which run lower in every system. England's own Type-1 performance is well below its all-types number. The two are not directly comparable — the console shows England to give a sense of scale and shared standards, not to rank the nations.

11 · Refreshing the data (CKAN)

To update the embedded snapshot, re-query the Public Health Scotland CKAN API (keyless) and rebuild. The console's own "Data health → How to refresh" panel carries the exact query; the core call is:

BASE=https://www.opendata.nhs.scot/api/3/action

# latest A&E week, by major (Type 1) ED:
GET $BASE/datastore_search_sql?sql=
  SELECT "TreatmentLocation",
         SUM("NumberOfAttendancesEpisode"),
         SUM("NumberWithin4HoursEpisode"),
         SUM("NumberOver12HoursEpisode")
  FROM "a5f7ca94-c810-41b5-a7c9-25c18d43e5a4"
  WHERE "WeekEndingDate" = (SELECT max("WeekEndingDate")
                            FROM "a5f7ca94-c810-41b5-a7c9-25c18d43e5a4")
    AND "DepartmentType" = 'Type 1'
    AND "AttendanceCategory" = 'All'
  GROUP BY "TreatmentLocation"

Resource IDs: A&E a5f7ca94… · sites lookup 1a4e3f48… · beds f272bb7d… · delayed discharges 49ca8a39…. The same datastore_search_sql endpoint serves all four. (If you ever want a truly live in-browser feed, route the CKAN call through a small CORS proxy — e.g. a Cloudflare Worker, the same approach used for live equities elsewhere.)

12 · Honest limits & appropriate use

  • Cadence, not real-time. Weekly (A&E) to quarterly (beds). This is a situational read, not a live operational monitor.
  • Descriptive, not predictive. No figure forecasts future performance; the scenarios are rehearsals, not predictions.
  • One week is noisy. Always read a single ED's week against the national trend.
  • Definitions matter. Type-1 (major ED) figures differ from all-types; cross-board and cross-country comparisons carry caveats.
  • Snapshot vintage. The console states its data dates; refresh per Section 11 for current figures.
Appropriate use: situational awareness and planning support for system, board and operational leaders, and a public-facing, fully-sourced picture of unscheduled-care pressure. Because it's built only on already-published PHS open data, it carries no confidentiality constraint.
