Common ≠ deadliest. Lung cancer is both the most common and by far the deadliest. Breast and prostate cancers are very common but far more survivable when caught early; liver, pancreatic and stomach cancers are less common but highly lethal. The gap between a cancer's incidence and its mortality reflects how treatable and how early-detected it is.

The mortality-to-incidence ratio is an interpretive proxy for survival, not a measured survival rate, and is sensitive to registry completeness (under-counted cases inflate it). The headline message — that survival depends heavily on where you live — is robust and consistent across sources (IARC, WHO).
◆ Prevention & early detection interpretive
A substantial share of cancers — by common estimates roughly 30–50% — are preventable by reducing known, modifiable causes; many more are curable when found early.
Interpretive synthesis of WHO / IARC guidance. Vaccination (HPV against cervical cancer; hepatitis B against liver cancer), tobacco control, and screening (cervical, breast, colorectal) are among the highest-impact measures, especially in lower-income settings where the survival gap is largest.
Source & method. IARC Global Cancer Observatory — GLOBOCAN 2022 (estimated incidence & mortality, all cancers; figures published 2024) and WHO. Estimates are modeled from national cancer registries with differing coverage; prevention figures and framing are interpretive. Numbers are rounded to the precision the estimates support.
