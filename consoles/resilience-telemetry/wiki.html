What this is — and what it is NOT

Read it: Start here. The console is explicitly NOT predictive. It is a stress thermometer, not a crash detector.
Does NOT mean: Do NOT use this to time trades or forecast crashes. Do NOT assume high readings mean a collapse is imminent. Do NOT treat lead-lag associations as causal relationships.

The Honesty Contract. This console describes financial stress in real time. It is not a crash predictor, not a timing tool, and makes no forecast. The framing borrows from physics (critical slowing down, phase transitions) as an analogy only — these signals are mostly absent before historical meltdowns.

Why Markets Aren't Like the Water System. The console's core panels — spectral radius, absorption ratio, turbulence, Hawkes clustering, lead-lag — were built as a thermometer of stress, not a thermometer of future crisis. By their own authors' admission, they rise during 2008/2020/2022, not before. Markets invert every advantage the water system has:

  • Topology is estimated and rewires in a crisis. The cross-asset network here is inferred from noisy returns; the instant volatility spikes, correlations collapse to one mode (everything moves together), and the graph rewires unpredictably.
  • Nothing is conserved. A crowded position unwinds in milliseconds through price. There is no quantity to integrate forward — no water level rising to a calculable overflow.
  • Drivers are endogenous and already priced. The would-be exogenous shocks (Fed policy, CPI, flows) are already embedded in price expectations. Only the unforecastable surprise remains.
  • Information has ~zero transport delay. Any stable upstream-leads-downstream pattern gets arbitraged away immediately. Lead-lag is an association observed in the data, not a causal edge you can exploit.
  • Price is the expectation itself. There is no hidden state. Watching the gauge perturbs it (reflexivity). The market is not a system with secrets to uncover.

What the Panels Actually Tell You. When the Fragility Thermometer rises (60–100), or spectral radius climbs, or Mahalanobis turbulence spikes, you are seeing that the market is currently moving in ways that are statistically large, persistent, or tightly coupled — today. A high reading can persist for months or years with nothing catastrophic happening. It can also drop from low levels with no warning. This is coincident, not leading.

The Only Weakly-Honest Leading Signal. The standardized absorption-ratio shift has shown the barest hint of lead in some sample windows — but it is sample-dependent and has not held out-of-sample. Do not trade on it.

The meta-learning reliability layer

Read it: A layer above the forecasts. It does not predict — it reports when each forecast can be trusted: a skill board (out-of-sample R² for 5- and 20-day volatility vs direction) and a regime-conditional reliability table (how forecastable volatility is in calm / normal / stressed regimes), with your current regime marked.
Does NOT mean: NOT a way to manufacture directional alpha. Conditioning on regime improves your sense of how much to trust and size the volatility/risk forecast — it never makes direction forecastable. Direction stays R²≈0 in every regime.

This is meta-labeling in its only honest form (after López de Prado): use the working risk model to gate and size, not to invent a directional signal. The regime table makes two facts concrete. First, volatility is far more forecastable when stress is already elevated — its regime-conditional R² climbs from roughly 0.01 in calm markets to ~0.07 normal to ~0.35 stressed (clustering is strongest in turmoil). Second, direction is unforecastable in every regime (R²≈0 throughout). So the honest use is: in a stressed regime, trust and act on the volatility/risk reading more; never read any of it as a direction call. The panel highlights which regime you are in right now.

Cascade simulation — shock propagation

Read it: Pick a market to shock and a minimum link strength; the panel propagates the shock downstream along the directed spillover network and reports how many markets it reaches, in how many waves, and which fall in each wave. It also reports the percolation threshold — the link strength below which a single shock spans the whole system.
Does NOT mean: NOT a forecast and NOT a claim that shocking X will hit Y. The network is estimated from noisy returns, it rewires in a crisis, and lead-lag is association arbitraged toward lag 0. This is a descriptive what-if drill on the current inferred structure.

This is your St. Pete water‑cascade mechanic — directed downstream propagation — brought to the market network as a directed‑reachability (percolation) cascade, the same family of model used for epidemics, power‑grid blackouts, and bank runs. A shock at the source flows to any market reachable via directed lead‑lag links of at least the chosen strength; each wave is one hop further out. Raising the trigger keeps only the strongest links (the cascade localises); lowering it lets weaker links carry the shock system‑wide. The behaviour is genuinely source‑dependent: a shock at a hub like the S&P spreads in a few waves across dozens of markets, while a shock at a peripheral market like agriculture dies almost immediately. The percolation line is the tipping point at which one shock spans ≥50% of the system — the cascade‑analysis read on systemic fragility. It is descriptive: it shows how the currently inferred structure would carry a shock, not whether one is coming.

Game-theoretic lens — beauty contest & coordination risk

Read it: Two things. First, the strategic reason short-term direction can't be forecast (Keynes' beauty contest / higher-order beliefs). Second, a coordination/run-vulnerability gauge built from existing readings — strategic complementarity (coupling), crowding (share of markets already stretched), and whether the system's leader (focal point) is stable.
Does NOT mean: NOT a prediction that a run or crash will occur. The run-vulnerability gauge is a global-games framing that reads conditions off the cross-asset state — it never claims a coordinated move is imminent.

This makes the game theory that was implicit in the console explicit. Why direction is a guessing game: a price is the market's average guess about everyone else's average guess (Keynes' beauty contest); acting on a public signal moves the very price you are predicting (reflexivity); and any stable edge is competed toward zero. That higher-order-belief loop is the game-theoretic reason short-term direction is a martingale — the console's whole thesis, restated in game theory. Coordination / run-vulnerability: a crisis is a coordination failure (a run, a stampede, a currency attack). Global-games theory (Morris–Shin) says such failures get likelier when actions strongly reinforce each other (high complementarity ≈ high coupling), many players already sit near their tipping point (crowding), and the focal point is shifting (an unstable system leader). The gauge blends those three into a descriptive vulnerability read — context for the regime, not a timing signal.

Network structure — hubs, choke points & amplification

Read it: Three structural reads of the directed spillover network: where outflow concentrates (preferential-attachment hubs), which market is the bottleneck whose removal most fragments how a shock spreads (choke point), and how far a typical shock travels (amplifier vs suppressor).
Does NOT mean: NOT a forecast. The network is estimated and rewires; these are descriptive structure of the current inference. The choke point is not a "weak link that will break" — it is where the current inferred topology happens to bottleneck.

This panel is the honest residue of two more cross-domain analogies — metabolic pathways and evolutionary/social graph theory. Preferential attachment (the rich-get-richer power law seen in wealth and in how well-connected mutant genes take over a structured population) shows up as spillover concentration: a Gini and top-3 share of out-strength, and the hub list. Choke points are the metabolic-pathway idea of bottleneck reactions: the market whose removal most fragments shock propagation — which is genuinely distinct from the loudest transmitter (in testing, the top transmitter was NVDA while the top choke points were MSFT and EM sovereign debt). Amplification is evolutionary graph theory's amplifier-vs-suppressor distinction: how far a single shock reaches on average. What deliberately did not make it: flux-balance analysis (the predictive core of metabolic modelling) needs a conserved, stoichiometric quantity — markets have none (their #2 missing ingredient); and fixation-probability / evolutionary-dynamics models need an observable population of agents or strategies, which price data does not contain. Importing either would manufacture prediction the substrate can't support — so only the descriptive network structure transfers.

Fragility thermometer

Read it: The Fragility thermometer is a per-asset panel with a hot/cold score (0–100) and six sub-scores (volatility, persistence, shocks, GARCH, AR shift, directedness), each with an explanatory caption. Highest scores sort to the top.
Does NOT mean: A high score is NOT a crash-prediction tool. It describes the asset's current stress signature: larger moves, less mean-reversion, more clustering. The console explicitly warns: 'DESCRIPTIVE, NOT PREDICTIVE… A resilience thermometer, not a crash detector… [it] can persist for months or years with nothing happening.'

The console's headline score: a composite 0–100 "thermal" reading across six descriptive stress channels, one score per asset.

The six channels: All are not independent; they overlap significantly, especially the persistence measures:

  1. Volatility surge: Realized volatility relative to asset-specific norm.
  2. Persistence / mean-reversion loss: Autocorrelation of returns (DFA / Hurst exponent α rising toward 1 = less mean-reversion, higher persistence).
  3. Shock magnitude: Frequency of large moves (≥2σ, ≥3σ thresholds).
  4. Volatility clustering (GARCH signal): Conditional heteroskedasticity.
  5. Absorption-ratio shift: Increase in AR from baseline (the only weakly-leading variant, though sample-dependent).
  6. Network asymmetry: The asset's directedness score (how much it leads/lags peers).

Score interpretation: A high reading (e.g., 70/100) means that asset's recent fluctuations are larger, more persistent, and more asymmetric than its own historical norm — a descriptive summary of current stress, not a forecast.

Behavior in the wild: High fragility readings can persist for months or years with nothing happening. Declines also routinely start from low readings, so a low score does not guarantee stability. The console is explicit: "A high reading can persist for months or years with nothing happening, and declines also start from low readings."

Honest caveats per channel:

  • Volatility: Mostly restates known GARCH clustering; often coincident, not leading.
  • Persistence (DFA): Needs long windows; descriptive, lags the regime shift.
  • Shock frequency: Coincident measure of tail activity.
  • GARCH: Confirms clustering, no predictive power.
  • AR shift: Weak regime flag; hasn't held out-of-sample.
  • Directedness: Association, not causation; nearly contemporaneous leads.

Why the critical-slowing-down analogy breaks: Physics systems (lasers, neurons) show genuine early warning via reduced variance and coherence before a bifurcation. Markets invert this: price is the expectation; there's no hidden state to telemeter. Correlations are estimated and rewire instantly on news, so no fixed topology exists until after the move. The console is unsparing: "The critical-slowing-down framing is an analogy from physics (these signals were largely absent before historical meltdowns); the volatility channel mostly restates GARCH."

Indicator detail — the six channels

Read it: /Users/patrickiben/Documents/Resilience_Telemetry/Resilience_Telemetry_Console.html lines 273–291 (fragilityFromReturns), lines 360–368 (INDMETA), lines 375–382 (drawIndic rendering)
Does NOT mean: Not a 6-dimensional voting system. Persistence overlaps across AC1/λ/DFA. Amplitude is driven by GARCH. Tail is one-event-sensitive. No weighting by predictive power—they are ensemble members, not independent classifiers.

Each fragility score rests on seven vol-standardized channels. They are not independent votes—persistence channels especially overlap. Displayed in walk-forward order: AC1, recovery rate, realized vol, bimodality, skewness, excess kurtosis, DFA.

Persistence (3 channels, averaged)

  • Lag-1 autocorrelation (CSD) — vol-standardized to avoid measuring GARCH. Rising = slower recovery from shocks. Weakest channel in markets; hard to move the needle.
  • Recovery rate λ = −ln φ (φ = AR(1) parameter) — same information as AC1, repackaged. Falling λ = slower return to equilibrium. A sibling, not an independent vote.
  • DFA / Hurst α — rising toward 1 = more persistence / less mean-reversion. Needs long windows (252-day rolling, computed every 3 days); descriptive, often lags the regime shift by weeks.

Amplitude (2 channels, averaged)

  • Realized volatility (annualized) — rising = larger fluctuations / higher stress. Mostly restates known volatility clustering (GARCH); often coincident, not leading. Daily realized vol is √(252 × variance of 252-day returns).
  • Bimodality / flickering — rising = switching between calm and stressed states. Vol clustering alone produces apparent switching; high false-positive rate. Computed from skewness, kurtosis, and sample size.

Tail (2 channels, averaged)

  • Skewness — more negative = asymmetry toward big drops. Returns are always left-skewed; one bad day flips the sign. Estimator is noisy.
  • Excess kurtosis — rising = fatter tails. Estimator dominated by 1–2 extreme days; very noisy. High variance, little signal between regimes in practice.

Each channel displays a small caveat in the console:

  • AC1: Weakest in markets; mostly noise in equities and FX.
  • Recovery rate: Same as AC1, not a separate vote.

  • Realized vol: GARCH-driven, not a unique transition signal.

Surprise lens (active-inference analogue)

Read it: /Users/patrickiben/Documents/Resilience_Telemetry/Resilience_Telemetry_Console.html lines 331–333 (computeSurprise), lines 388–393 (drawSurprise rendering)
Does NOT mean: Not a leading indicator. Not volatility or variance—it is a residual innovation metric conditioned on a 60-day local model. Not a forecast of returns or crashes. Not independent of fragility—both rise during stress, but neither causes the other.

Core Concept

One-step negative log-likelihood (NLL) from a simple AR(1) + EWMA model. Analogue of variational free energy from active inference: higher surprise = larger deviation from the asset's local predictive model. Reported as a percentile (0–100 normalized against the asset's rolling 20-day mean).

Calculation

For each day t ≥ 1:

  • Fit AR(1) model φ on a rolling 60-day window ending at t−1; estimate its variance v.
  • Predict next day: μ = φ × r[t−1].
  • Compute NLL: 0.5 × ((r[t] − μ) / σ)² + log(σ), where σ = √v.
  • Update variance via EWMA: v ← 0.94 × v + 0.06 × (r[t] − μ)².
  • Smooth with 20-day rolling average.
  • Convert to percentile using mean and std of the rolling series.

Interpretation

High surprise (70–100) = asset behavior deviates far from its own 60-day predictive model. Not a forecast signal, but a coincident read on how much the asset is surprising itself. During stress (2020, 2022), surprise tends to rise, along with vol and fragility.

Why "Active Inference"?

In active inference, the system minimizes surprise (prediction error) to maintain a generative model. Here, we invert it: surprise measures how much the asset's local model is failing. Rising surprise = environment is drifting away from the model, not that a crash is imminent.

Directed spillover network (LCCA) — who leads whom

Read it: /Users/patrickiben/Documents/Resilience_Telemetry/Resilience_Telemetry_Console.html lines 246–259 (lcca, gatePairThresh), lines 395–430 (drawNetwork, drawHeat, drawNetBars), lines 116–127 (HTML captions)
Does NOT mean: Not causal graphs. Not a predictor of spillover events. Not a ranking of systemic importance—transmitters may be volatility conduits, not fundamental drivers. The network rewires in crises; historical in-sample links often don't hold out-of-sample. Lead-lag is dominated by common risk factors and information lags, not structural causality.

What Is LCCA?

Lagged Cross-Correlation Asymmetry: measures which asset's past returns forecast another's future better. An arrow i→j means i's lagged returns correlate more strongly with j's forward returns than the reverse, over the 252-day analysis window.

Calculation

For each pair i, j and lags k = 1 to L (L = 4 days):

  • Compute Fisher z-transformed cross-correlation: xcorr(i, j, k) at lag k.
  • Sum absolute z-values: D(i→j) = Σ|fz(xcorr(i, j, k))| / L.
  • Asymmetry: A(i→j) = D(i→j) − D(j→i). Red i→j means i leads j; blue j leads i; near-white = pure co-movement.
  • Net transmitter/receiver: NET(i) = Σⱼ A(i→j). Right = transmitter (red), left = receiver (blue).

Permutation Gate

Only links pass if they exceed a 95th-percentile threshold from a circular-shift null (160 random rotations per asset pair). Solid arrows = significant under the gate; faint dashed = strongest tendencies below significance.

Directedness Ratio

Σ|A| / ΣD—how much of the total cross-correlation is directional (asymmetric) vs co-movement. Higher = more spillover; lower = assets moving as one. Mostly stays <0.5 in normal periods, peaks during crises.

Critical Honesty Note

Lead-lag is association, not causation. Most surviving leads peak at 1 day (≈contemporaneous), reflecting information diffusion and microstructure, not causal transmission. In backtest, you can inject synthetic leads to watch the network respond—but real lead-lag tendencies are fleeting and don't generalize out-of-sample. The network is a snapshot of dependence structure, not a transmission mechanism.

Display

  • Network diagram: nodes sized by |NET|, positioned by transmitter/receiver rank. Edges show top 12 absolute asymmetries.
  • Heat matrix: rows = source i, cols = target j. Red (i→j) = i leads; blue = j leads i. Intensity = strength of the z-transformed correlation asymmetry.
  • Net bars: sorted by NET, showing TO (total outflows) and FROM (total inflows) per asset.

Asymmetry matrix & net transmitter / receiver

Read it: The N×N heatmap (rows = source, columns = target) shows which asset leads which. The bar chart to the right ranks all assets by net directedness: red extends right (transmits), blue extends left (receives).
Does NOT mean: The values are correlation-based associations, not causal drivers. A red SPY→LQD link does not mean stock strength causes bond weakness; it means their lagged returns moved together in that direction during the window, and that relationship is statistically above random circular-shift shuffles.

The left panel shows the directed asymmetry matrix A(i→j) = D(i→j) − D(j→i), where D(i→j) is the sum of absolute lagged cross-correlations of i leading j.

Matrix interpretation:

  • Red cells (positive A) indicate i→j: asset i leads asset j.
  • Blue cells (negative A) indicate j→i: asset j leads asset i.
  • Near-white cells indicate pure co-movement with no directional winner (both lead/lag symmetrically).

The matrix is anti-symmetric: if A(SPY→LQD) is red, then A(LQD→SPY) is equally blue.

Right panel — net directional spillover: A horizontal bar chart ranks assets by their NET score (sum of all outgoing leads minus all incoming lags):

  • Right/red bars: Pure sources and hubs that lead more than they lag.
  • Left/blue bars: Pure sinks and relays that lag more than they lead.
  • TO[i] = total outgoing lead-lag contribution (i leads others).
  • FROM[i] = total incoming lead-lag contribution (others lead i).

Typical findings: broad equity indices (SPY, QQQ) often sit near neutral; commodities and credit tend more blue (receiver); volatility (VIX) and FX can oscillate.

Systemic coupling — spectral radius ρ̂ & absorption ratio

Read it: The 'Systemic coupling' panel shows two sparklines and current values: ρ̂ (spectral radius) and AR (absorption ratio). Both are plotted over time; the caveats explain they rise DURING crises (2008, 2020, 2022), not before.
Does NOT mean: These are NOT early-warning signals of a crash. They are 'thermometer' readings of how unified the market currently is, analogous to critical-slowing-down in physics, but the analogy breaks in markets because the topology is estimated post-hoc, not fixed as in water systems.

These two metrics measure how tightly the whole cross-asset system is moving as a single unified entity. Both are coincident-to-lagging indicators: they spike during stress, not before.

Spectral radius ρ̂: The dominant eigenvalue (λ_max) of the rolling cross-asset correlation matrix, divided by the number of assets. Think of it as a proxy for how many independent modes of variation remain in the system. High ρ̂ (closer to 1) means the assets are "mode-collapsing" — correlations rising, individual shock absorption falling.

  • Formula: ρ̂ = λ_max / N (where N is the asset count).
  • This is an analogy to the VAR spectral radius ρ(Φ) from your seismic/hydraulic work, not a fitted dynamical operator.
  • The console uses macro/equity/FX assets only (crypto excluded) to maintain clean history back to 2016.

Absorption ratio (AR): The fraction of total variance explained by the top few eigenmodes (usually 5–10). High AR = little local shock absorption; most of the move is concentrated in the leading modes. Kritzman-Li (2011) framed this as a fragility measure, but it too rises during crises, not before.

Why they are not leading indicators: Both metrics are fixed-topology readouts of realized correlation. When liquidity evaporates or systematic factors dominate (as in March 2020 or September 2022), all correlations spike simultaneously — the topology doesn't exist until after the move. The console's transparency: "ρ̂ is an analogy to the water system's fixed topology — except here the topology is estimated and rewires in a crisis."

What they tell you: High ρ̂/AR indicates the current market is moving in lock-step (regime of tight coupling), not that a break is imminent. A persistent high reading can last months or years with nothing happening; declines also start from low readings (base-rate honesty).

Anticipation — where to act, & the out-of-sample test (the predictive layer)

The engine splits history into a train half (first 60%) and a held-out test half (last 40%). It diagnoses the transmitter on the train half only, then checks on the unseen test half whether that transmitter's stress actually leads the basket's downstream stress — and by how much versus the loudest market. It reports the lead as a correlation and how robust it is across bootstrap resamples.

This mirrors the paper's own real-data result, which was positive but not statistically significant. Expect a small lead: it confirms the transmitter is genuinely upstream, but the absolute predictability is low — exactly why the panel refuses to call it a trade signal. Financial turbulence (Mahalanobis)

Read it: /Users/patrickiben/Documents/Resilience_Telemetry/Resilience_Telemetry_Console.html lines 491–503 (computeTurbulence), lines 536–538 (drawTurbulence rendering and caveats), lines 141–146 (HTML caption on turbulence)
Does NOT mean: Not a forecast of volatility or crashes. Not a threshold-crossing predictor—high turbulence can persist for days with no event, or spike on a single outlier. Not independent of spectral radius; both are multivariate stress measures that correlate during crises but neither causes the other. χ² tails are understated in financial returns.

Core Measure

The Mahalanobis distance of today's 252-day cross-asset return vector from its mean, conditioned on the rolling covariance matrix. A multivariate extension of the per-asset surprise lens, capturing how far the system sits from its typical state in metric-adjusted space.

Calculation

  • Estimate mean μ and covariance Σ from the 252-day window of N-asset returns.
  • Regularize Σ with shrinkage (Ledoit-Wolf, α ≈ 0.2) to avoid ill-conditioning.
  • Compute D² = (x − μ)ᵀ Σ⁻¹ (x − μ) for today's 10-day aggregate return vector x.
  • Map to χ² CDF percentile using N degrees of freedom.
  • Smooth with 20-day rolling average for trend.

Interpretation

Higher turbulence = larger deviation from the system's covariance-weighted center. Rising turbulence during 2020 and 2022 crises is well-documented; it is a coincident-to-slightly-lagging read on regime stress, not a leading signal.

The drivers panel shows each asset's contribution to today's Mahalanobis distance: xᵢ × (Σ⁻¹ x)ᵢ. Rank by magnitude to see which assets are pushing the system away from its center.

Caveat: Fat Tails & χ² Optimism

χ² percentiles are optimistic in financial fat tails—the true tail probability is worse. Treat the percentile as ordinal (higher = worse), not as a precise exceedance probability. During high turbulence, the true risk is underestimated.

Display

Top section: current Mahalanobis D² as a 0–100 percentile bar, with rolling sparkline. Below: top contributors (assets with largest influence on the distance).

Shock clustering — Hawkes self-excitation

Read it: /Users/patrickiben/Documents/Resilience_Telemetry/Resilience_Telemetry_Console.html lines 504–549 (fitHawkes, computeHawkes, drawHawkes rendering), lines 192–193 (HTML caption on Hawkes)
Does NOT mean: Not a predictor of the next shock. Not a measure of systemic importance or contagion. High branching ratio = feedback loops are active, not that a crash is coming. n is very noisy with few events; a single bad day changes the fit significantly. Not independent of turbulence or fragility—all three rise together during crises, but none causes the others.

Core Concept

Do large moves beget more large moves? Fits a self-exciting point process to large return shocks (≥2.5σ magnitude). The branching ratio n is how self-reinforcing the clustering is: n ≈ 0.3 means shocks decay; n ≈ 0.9 means critical (on the brink of runaway); n = 1 is unstable.

Calculation

  • For each asset: identify days with |return| ≥ 2.5σ (threshold 2.5 from the window). Record these as event times.
  • Fit Hawkes process: λ(t) = μ + α Σ exp(−β(t − tᵢ)), where μ = background rate, α = jump size, β = decay rate.
  • Grid search over (μ, α, β) to maximize log-likelihood; then refine locally.
  • Compute branching ratio: n = α exp(−β) / (1 − exp(−β)). This is the expected number of children per event in a Galton-Watson branching process.
  • Also report half-life: how fast aftershocks decay (log(2) / β).

Interpretation

CONTEMPORANEOUS: high n = shocks CURRENTLY clustering (aftershocks underway), NOT a coming exogenous mainshock. Rising branching ratio during stress (2022) means the cascade is self-sustaining. However, a high n does not forecast the next shock's magnitude or timing—only that if a shock occurs, more are likely to follow quickly. Implements your LSAxJEPA Hawkes λᵢ(t); the temporal twin of ρ(Φ) → 1 (cf. the induced-seismicity pilot).

Caveats

  • Least-stable panel: single exponential kernel, daily bins, often few jump events (≤5). Confidence in n is low.
  • Threshold-dependent: moving 2.5σ to 3.0σ or 2.0σ changes the count and fit.
  • Temporal resolution: daily bins may miss intra-day clustering and aftershock sequences that resolve in hours.

Display

Current branching ratio n as a 0–100 bar chart (clipped at 1.0 for display). Per-asset breakdown: count of shocks, estimated n, and half-life in days. A message appears if too few shocks are detected to fit a reliable process.

Overlapping asset coverage

The network and directed-spillover panels compute on the union of available assets for each date. The systemic coupling (spectral radius/absorption) drops out if fewer than 3×N days are available for the current asset subset; the manifold requires sufficient 10-day block states to cluster.

Why it borrows the water dashboard's lenses but not its foresight

Read it: Markets have no fixed topology, no accumulation, no exogenous drivers, and no hidden state. Every structural advantage the water system has for forecasting is inverted in markets.
Does NOT mean: Do not treat elevated readings as predictive signals. They show current stress, not future moves.

The Resilience Telemetry console applies several structural analogs from the Saint Petersburg SCADA water-monitoring system (which can warn hours ahead). However, four critical market differences prevent the same forward-looking skill:

1. Fixed topology vs. estimated and rewiring

Water: The pipe network and elevation structure are ground-truth, surveyed and stable. Pressure and flow respond to known fixed constraints.
Markets: The cross-asset correlation network is estimated from noisy daily returns and systematically rewires when a crisis hits. Historical "lead-lag" dissolves; everything converges to one mode (panic). The topology is a ghost until it breaks.

2. Conservation vs. price instantaneity

Water: Water piles up in tanks of known volume and travels pipes at known speed. Total level + rate-of-rise → calculable time-to-overflow.
Markets: Nothing accumulates. A crowded position unwinds in milliseconds through price. There is no quantity to integrate forward; only the expectation of future supply/demand is priced. A crowded trade can evaporate before any aggregating metric signals it.

3. Exogenous drivers with real lead time vs. endogenous and already priced

Water: Rain and surge are exogenous — coming whether or not anyone forecasts them, and don't change course because operators reacted to yesterday's forecast.
Markets: The "drivers" (Fed policy, CPI, flows) are already priced into expectations by the time they become observable. Information arbitrage across venues means any stable upstream-leads-downstream edge is traded to lag zero. Surprise is all that remains — by definition, unforecastable.

4. Hidden-state telemetry vs. price as the expectation

Water: SCADA reads a tank level only the operator can see. The gauge is independent of what happens next; observing it does not change the water level.
Markets: Price is the aggregate expectation. There is no hidden state to telemeter. Worse, observing a gauge — discussing a risk, hedging it, trading on it — immediately changes the expectation and the price. Markets exhibit reflexivity; water systems do not.

Consequence: thermometer, not forecast

The spectral radius (ρ̂), absorption ratio, turbulence, Hawkes branching ratio, and timed lead-lag are built as a systemic-stress thermometer. By their authors' own admission, these metrics rise during 2008, 2020, and 2022 — not before. They are coincident-to-lagging measures of stress, not predictive. The only weakly-leading construct is the standardized absorption-ratio shift, and even that is sample-dependent and has not held out-of-sample.

This is exactly why everything here stays descriptive, not predictive. The console tells you where the market sits today, in geometric and spectral terms, relative to its own history — not where it will go.

FAQ for investors & operators

Read it: High readings persist for years without crashing. Lead-lag is association, not causation. Closest analogs are geometric, not predictive. Descriptive, not predictive.
Does NOT mean: This is not a black-box trading system or a crash-prediction tool. It is a diagnostic for understanding current market stress.

Q: Is a red reading (fragility ≥70) a sell signal?

A: No. A high fragility reading is a descriptive statement: "This asset's recent fluctuations are larger, more persistent, or more asymmetric than its own historical norm." It does not predict when a drawdown will occur. Readings can stay red for months or years without significant correction; conversely, crashes sometimes begin from low readings. This is a stress thermometer, not a market-timing tool.

Q: I've seen elevated readings for months with nothing happening. Does that mean the console is broken?

A: No, that is expected and honest. With only a handful of real drawdown events in a decade, the base-rate chance of a crash on any given day is already low. An elevated thermometer reading raises that chance somewhat, but from a very small number. The Backtest / base-rate honesty panel quantifies this: if you find that a fragility ≥70 raises the 10-day forward drawdown rate from (say) 1% to 1.8%, that is the true signal — modest and uncertain. The console will never claim otherwise.

Q: Does an arrow in the network mean A causes B?

A: No. An arrow i→j means i's past returns have a higher predictive weight for j's future than the reverse. This is called "lead-lag" or "Granger causality" in econometrics, but it is purely correlational. Causes can include: genuine information cascades (rare), different market-open times, measurement lag, or simple overfitting. Any stable edge gets arbitraged away; what you see is mostly the noise of the week.

Q: Is the "closest-analog" state a prediction?

A: No. The diffusion map shows that today's state is geometrically closest to (say) 2019 or 2021. That historical state is a coincident analog — it looked similar. But it does not predict that the same moves will follow. Markets have short memory, regime shifts can happen overnight, and new shocks are always possible.

Q: What does it mean if spectral radius is rising?

A: It means the cross-asset correlation matrix is concentrating its variance in fewer eigenmodes. Assets are moving more in sync. This is a real, measurable signal of systemic tightness — but it is a coincident signal, visible during 2020 and 2022 as the crisis unfolded, not cleanly ahead of time. Rising coupling makes the system more fragile to the next shock, but the shock's timing and magnitude are unforecastable.

Q: If the Hawkes branching ratio approaches 1, is a crash imminent?

A: No. A branching ratio near 1 (critical coupling) means large shocks are currently self-reinforcing — they beget more large shocks. This is a real description of the shock cascade active right now. But it does not tell you how long the cascade will last, how large the next shock will be, or when it will end. You are observing the stress, not predicting its duration.

Q: What if all the indicators are red and the diffusion map shows the stressed arm?

A: That is a coherent stress signal: the market is exhibiting high fragility, tight coupling, strong shock clustering, and a geometric regime similar to past crises. This is valuable information for risk managers, forcing you to review your hedge ratios, concentration, and liquidity assumptions. It does not tell you to sell everything immediately, nor does it guarantee a crash by Friday. It is an honest call to heightened vigilance.

Q: Can I backtest a strategy on these signals?

A: Yes, and the console offers a Backtest / research mode with strict walk-forward recomputation and base-rate honesty tables. But be warned: with a dozen real drawdown events in 10 years, apparent backtest "skill" is mostly luck. The console compares your fragility-based signal to the unconditional base rate and shows confidence intervals. Treat any edge as weak and prone to overfitting until it is validated out-of-sample on new data.

Q: Is the volatility channel different from GARCH?

A: Not meaningfully. Realized volatility (the amplitude sub-score) and the bimodality coefficient mostly restate volatility clustering, which GARCH models directly. If you run a GARCH on the same asset, you will see similar conditional-vol spikes. The surprise lens (AR(1)+EWMA-vol log-likelihood) is more independent, but still a simple model. Neither the volatility channel nor surprise are strong out-of-sample predictors of drawdowns.

Q: What does the covariance shrinkage α do in the turbulence panel?

A: It blends the sample covariance matrix with the identity (uniform correlation) to reduce estimation noise when data is scarce or dimensions are high. Higher α = more shrinkage toward identity. The default (0.20) is conservative. If covariance becomes ill-conditioned (nearly singular), α auto-raises. Adjust the slider to see how turbulence readings change with shrinkage, but remember: turbulence itself is a coincident metric, not forward-looking.

Q: Can I use this for real-time trading?

A: Only if your risk tolerance aligns with "descriptive of current stress, not a market timer." You might use elevated fragility and tight coupling to tighten stops, increase hedging, or reduce leverage — all risk-management moves. Trying to trade the direction or timing based on these signals will likely fail out-of-sample. The console is for situational awareness, not alpha generation.

Glossary

Read it: See definitions of all key terms: spectral radius, absorption ratio, fragility thermometer, LCCA, diffusion map, Hawkes, turbulence, surprise, DFA, bimodality, and more.
Does NOT mean: These are technical terms; understanding them is important for interpreting the console correctly and avoiding false signals.
Absorption ratio
The fraction of total variance in a correlation matrix captured by the top k eigenmodes (Kritzman-Li metric). High ratio = little local shock-absorbing capacity; all variance is driven by a shared global mode. Coincident, not leading.
AC1 (Lag-1 autocorrelation)
Correlation between today's return and yesterday's return. In efficient markets, ~0. Rising values suggest slower mean-reversion (critical-slowing-down analogue). Weak in returns; mostly restated by volatility clustering.
Backtest / research mode
Walk-forward computation: at any cursor date, all statistics use only data up to that date. The panel then compares actual forward drawdowns to the base rate. Strict honesty check against luck.
Base-rate honesty
A statistical comparison of the actual frequency of drawdowns when fragility ≥70 (conditional rate) vs. any random day (unconditional base rate). If conditional ≈ unconditional, there is no demonstrated timing skill.
Bimodality coefficient
Statistical measure of "two-humped" distribution shape. Rising bimodality suggests the asset is flickering between calm and stressed states. Often a false positive driven by GARCH vol clustering alone.
Branching ratio (Hawkes)
In a Hawkes self-exciting point process, the expected number of child shocks per parent shock. Ratio ~1 = critical (approaching instability); <<1 = shocks die away fast. Realizes your LSAxJEPA temporal Hawkes λi(t).
Critical-slowing-down (CSD)
In physics, the analogue of rising recovery time, persistence, and amplification near a phase transition. These metrics (AC1, DFA, rvol, bimodality) borrow the analogy from CSD, but in markets they rise during stress, not before, and are largely coincident or even lagging.
Cross-correlation asymmetry (LCCA)
Matrix A(i→j) = D(i→j) − D(j→i), where D is Granger causality direction (past i→future j). Anti-symmetric: red when i leads j, blue when j leads i. Predictive association, not causation.
Diffusion map
A nonlinear dimensionality-reduction technique (generalization of spectral decomposition / PCA) that embeds high-dimensional states (e.g., 20-asset returns over 10 days) into a low-D space so that similar regimes cluster. Realizes the geometric manifold of your LSAxJEPA framework.
DFA (Detrended fluctuation analysis) / Hurst exponent α
Measures scaling of cumulative returns; α ≈0.5 = random walk (no persistence), α >0.5 = more persistence (slow mean-reversion), α <0.5 = mean-reverting. Needs long windows; lags regime shifts; mostly descriptive.
Directedness ratio (Σ|A|/ΣD)
A network statistic: sum of absolute asymmetries divided by total Granger causality magnitude. Low ratio = network is becoming less directional (everything leads everything); high ratio = clear leader-follower structure. Collapses in crises.
Eigenvalue (λmax)
The dominant (largest) eigenvalue of a correlation matrix. Normalized by N (number of assets), it gives the spectral radius ρ̂. Dominance = systemic coherence; in crises, ρ̂ rises as assets correlate to one mode.
Fragility thermometer
A per-asset 0–100 score combining three channels (persistence, amplitude, tail) that measure how far recent behavior sits above the asset's own historical norm. High ≠ crash; can persist for years. Descriptive, not predictive.
Granger causality / lead-lag
Statistical test of whether past values of X help forecast Y more than X's own past alone. "Lead" means X's past is predictive for Y's future; "lag" means the reverse. In markets, this is association driven by mechanics, measurement lag, and overfitting, not true causation.
Hawkes process
A self-exciting point process where arrivals (shocks) increase the intensity of future arrivals (branching ratio n). Fitted to daily return shocks ≥2.5σ; branching ratio rises in stress. Realizes your LSAxJEPA temporal shock-clustering λi(t) framework.
Ill-conditioned covariance
When a covariance matrix is nearly singular (near-zero or negative eigenvalues), inverse estimates blow up. Shrinkage α or dimension reduction is applied. The console auto-raises α if the matrix becomes ill-conditioned.
Mahalanobis distance / turbulence
One-day multivariate generalization of z-score. Measures how unusual today's cross-asset move is relative to the recent covariance. Risen in 2020/2022 (coincident), not before. An active-inference analogue (variational free energy) of per-asset surprise.
Manifold / state geometry
The high-dimensional space spanned by historical market states (e.g., 20-asset 10-day returns). A diffusion map embeds it into 2-D; similar regimes cluster. Coincident description of the market's regime geometry, not predictive of next state.
Permutation-gated null
Statistical test: reorder (shuffle) the returns of asset j to break any causal link to i, then recompute lead-lag. Only keep links i→j that exceed the null distribution. Filters spurious edges but is conservative.
Realized volatility (rvol)
Square root of the sum of squared daily returns over a window, annualized. A measure of magnitude of fluctuations. Rising rvol mostly restates GARCH vol clustering; a coincident metric, not predictive.
Recovery rate λ = −ln φ
Derived from AR(1) coefficient φ. λ measures the rate of exponential reversion to mean; rising λ = faster recovery. Sibling of AC1; largely redundant information in markets.
Shrinkage (α)
Regularization parameter for covariance estimation: C_shrink = (1−α)×C_sample + α×I. Higher α = more smoothing, better condition number, but less responsiveness to true covariance changes. Default 0.20; auto-raised if ill-conditioned.
Snapshot (embedded real history)
10-year pre-computed time series of equities, FX, commodities, credit, rates, and vol indices (starting 2016); ~2.7 years of crypto. Baked into the HTML; always available even if live feeds fail.
Spectral radius ρ̂
The dominant eigenvalue of the rolling cross-asset correlation matrix, normalized by N. Measures systemic coupling: ρ̂→1 = tight coupling (all move together); ρ̂ low = loose coupling (independent shocks). Coincident stress metric, not predictive. Your LSAxJEPA κB analogue.
Surprise lens (active-inference analogue)
One-step negative log-likelihood of a simple AR(1)+EWMA-volatility model. Rising = today's moves are more surprising to a simple expectation model. An active-inference variational-free-energy analogue, not the full EFE engine. Per-asset, not systemic.
Walk-forward validation
Strict backtesting methodology: at any in-sample date, all parameters are estimated using only data up to that date. Forward metrics are computed on future data. Prevents look-ahead bias. The console's Backtest mode does this recomputation.
