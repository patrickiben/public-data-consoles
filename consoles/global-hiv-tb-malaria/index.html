HIV, TB & Malaria — the big threeThe three great communicable-disease epidemics: global trends since 2000, country burden, the income & regional divide, and country profiles

How to read this. Every figure is a World Bank reported value for a specific year — always shown next to the number, because the latest available year varies (often 2024 for the headline burden series, sometimes earlier for bed-net surveys or TB treatment outcomes). This is descriptive: it shows where the three epidemics stand and how they have moved since 2000. It does not predict the future. The big picture is two-sided — major long-run progress in all three, but recent plateauing, and a burden still heavily concentrated in sub-Saharan Africa.

Data & method. Source: World Bank Open Data — World Development Indicators, pulled live from the public Indicators API v2 (api.worldbank.org/v2) when reachable, otherwise from an embedded snapshot whose vintage is shown above. Indicators: HIV prevalence 15–49 SH.DYN.AIDS.ZS; TB incidence SH.TBS.INCD; malaria incidence SH.MLR.INCD.P3; TB treatment success SH.TBS.CURE.ZS; new adult HIV infections SH.HIV.INCD; children living with HIV SH.HIV.0014; insecticide-treated bed-net use SH.MLR.NETS.ZS. The World Bank compiles these from UNAIDS (HIV), the WHO Global Tuberculosis Programme and the WHO Global Malaria Programme.
Honesty. All numbers are measured World Bank reported values — but the latest available year differs by country and indicator, so it is always displayed; HIV prevalence, malaria incidence and bed-net use are only reported where the epidemic exists, so high-income groups and malaria-free regions correctly show "—" (not zero). Aggregates are the Bank's population-weighted estimates, not simple country averages. The choice of which countries and indicators to show is interpretive framing. This tool is descriptive, not predictive; it does not forecast. No personal or protected health information is used — only public country- and aggregate-level data.
