Antimicrobial ResistanceThe global & U.S. burden of drug-resistant bacteria and fungi — the "silent pandemic" — and an emerging-resistance watch

Why this one has no live feed. Unlike overdoses or respiratory viruses, there is no public, real-time AMR data stream. The authoritative numbers come from large periodic studies — the GRAM / IHME global burden estimates and the CDC Antibiotic Resistance Threats report — which are modeled estimates for a given year. They are embedded here with their vintage and source. This is honest surveillance, not a ticker.

⚠ Emerging-resistance watch interpretive
Resistance mechanisms and organisms spreading faster than the headline reports capture. Curated from CDC, WHO and published surveillance — qualitative early-warning, not a live count.
Compiled from CDC, WHO and peer-reviewed surveillance; reflects status as of the date below. Candida auris clinical-case figures are CDC surveillance counts (a rare AMR threat that is nationally reported) and show the steep recent rise; other entries are qualitative. Naloxone-style note: many of these have few or no remaining reliable treatments, which is what makes them "urgent".
◍ What drives it — & the broken pipeline interpretive
Interpretive synthesis of the scientific consensus (CDC, WHO, O'Neill Review 2016). AMR is a natural evolutionary process accelerated by how antimicrobials are used in people, animals and agriculture; the response combines stewardship, infection prevention, surveillance, and new drug/vaccine development.
Sources & method. Global: GRAM — Murray et al., Lancet 2022 (2019 burden) and GBD 2021 AMR Collaborators, Lancet 2024 (projection). U.S.: CDC Antibiotic Resistance Threats in the United States, 2019. C. auris: CDC surveillance. Drivers: CDC / WHO / O'Neill Review (2016).
