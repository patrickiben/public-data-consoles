Unofficial · descriptive WASH atlas, not a forecast. Built on the World Bank's open Indicators API (WHO/UNICEF JMP source data). The latest available year differs by country and indicator, and many smaller countries have data gaps. Not for clinical, individual, or policy decisions.

How to read this. Every figure is a World Bank reported value for a specific year — always shown next to the number, because the latest available year varies by country and indicator. This is descriptive: it shows where WASH access stands and how it has moved since 2000. It does not predict the future. Unsafe water and sanitation are major drivers of diarrheal disease and cholera — see the note in the headline panel and the companion Outbreak Tracker.

Data & method. Source: World Bank Open Data — World Development Indicators, pulled live from the public Indicators API v2 (api.worldbank.org/v2) when reachable, otherwise from an embedded snapshot whose vintage is shown above. Indicators: safely managed drinking water SH.H2O.SMDW.ZS; safely managed sanitation SH.STA.SMSS.ZS; basic handwashing facility SH.STA.HYGN.ZS; at-least-basic drinking water SH.H2O.BASW.ZS; at-least-basic sanitation SH.STA.BASS.ZS; open defecation SH.STA.ODFC.ZS. The World Bank compiles these from the WHO/UNICEF Joint Monitoring Programme (JMP) for Water Supply, Sanitation and Hygiene.
Honesty. Coverage and prevalence figures are measured World Bank reported values — but the latest available year differs by country and indicator, so it is always displayed; missing values are shown as "—" rather than guessed (an open-defecation value of 0% is a reported zero, not a gap). The "people still without" counts are modeled: coverage gap × the World Bank's 2024 world population estimate (~8.14 billion, SP.POP.TOTL); WHO/UNICEF JMP publishes comparable headline figures. The choice of which ~55 countries and 6 indicators to show, and the framing, is interpretive. This tool is descriptive, not predictive; it does not forecast. No personal or protected health information is used — only public country-level aggregates.
Why this matters. Unsafe drinking water, inadequate sanitation and poor hygiene are leading drivers of diarrheal disease and a precondition for cholera transmission. This atlas describes exposure context, not outbreaks — see the companion Outbreak Tracker for reported case activity.
