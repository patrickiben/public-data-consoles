How to read this. Each figure is a WHO/UNICEF coverage estimate reported through the World Bank for a specific year — always shown next to the number, because the latest available year varies by country. This is descriptive: it shows where coverage stands and how it has moved since 2000, including the dip around 2020–2021 and the partial recovery since. It does not predict future outbreaks. The ~95% reference line for measles is the commonly cited two-dose herd-immunity threshold; coverage below it is shown as interpretive resurgence-risk framing, not a forecast.

Data & method. Source: World Bank Open Data — World Development Indicators, pulled live from the public Indicators API v2 (api.worldbank.org/v2) when reachable, otherwise from an embedded snapshot whose vintage is shown above. Indicators: DTP3 SH.IMM.IDPT; measles 1st dose SH.IMM.MEAS; HepB3 SH.IMM.HEPB; newborns protected against tetanus SH.VAC.TTNS.ZS. The World Bank compiles these from the WHO/UNICEF Estimates of National Immunization Coverage (WUENIC).
Honesty. Coverage values are measured World Bank reported estimates — but the latest available year differs by country and indicator, so it is always displayed; many smaller economies have gaps, and missing values are shown as "—" rather than guessed. Aggregates are the Bank's population-weighted estimates. The choice of which ~63 countries and 4 vaccines to show, the "lowest-coverage-is-highest-risk" ordering, the "under-immunized = 100% − coverage" framing (no clean zero-dose indicator exists in this API), and the ~95% measles herd-immunity reference line are all interpretive framing. This tool is descriptive, not predictive; coverage below a threshold indicates elevated resurgence risk, not a forecast of any outbreak. No personal or protected health information is used — only public country-level aggregates.
