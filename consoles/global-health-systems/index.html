How to read this. Every figure is a World Bank reported value for a specific year — always shown next to the number, because the latest available year varies (financing series often run 2021–2023; physicians, nurses and beds can be a few years older). This is descriptive: it shows where systems stand and how spending and capacity have moved since 2000. It does not predict the future, and cross-country comparisons carry real differences in measurement, accounting and completeness.

Data & method. Source: World Bank Open Data — World Development Indicators / Global Health Expenditure, pulled live from the public Indicators API v2 (api.worldbank.org/v2) when reachable, otherwise from an embedded snapshot whose vintage is shown above. Indicators: health spending per capita SH.XPD.CHEX.PC.CD; health spending % GDP SH.XPD.CHEX.GD.ZS; government health spending % GDP SH.XPD.GHED.GD.ZS; out-of-pocket share SH.XPD.OOPC.CH.ZS; physicians SH.MED.PHYS.ZS; nurses & midwives SH.MED.NUMW.P3; hospital beds SH.MED.BEDS.ZS. The World Bank in turn compiles these from the WHO Global Health Expenditure Database, WHO/national workforce accounts and national statistics.
Honesty. All numbers are measured World Bank reported values — but the latest available year differs by country and indicator, so it is always displayed; many smaller economies have gaps, and missing values are shown as "—" rather than guessed (never zero). Aggregates are the Bank's population-weighted estimates. The choice of which ~55 countries and 7 indicators to show, and the framing of "spending vs. outcomes", is interpretive. The UHC service-coverage index (SH.UHC.SRVS.CV.XD) was retired from the API and is not embedded — see the country-profile note. This tool is descriptive, not predictive; it does not forecast. No personal or protected health information is used — only public country-level aggregates.
