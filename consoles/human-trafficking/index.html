If you suspect trafficking — what to do

Do not confront a suspected trafficker, and do not try to “rescue” anyone yourself. Doing so can put the victim — and you — in greater danger, and can disrupt a later investigation. Source: DHS Blue Campaign.
  1. Immediate danger? Call 911.
  2. Report a tip to the ICE HSI Tip Line 1‑866‑347‑2423, or anonymously to the National Hotline.
  3. Help a victim by sharing the National Human Trafficking Hotline: 1‑888‑373‑7888 / text 233733. Let them reach out on their own terms.
  4. Preserve their safety and choices. Survivors are best supported when their autonomy is respected.

Three very different kinds of numbers — don’t mix them up

Estimate
Modeled totals of how many people are affected (ILO/Walk Free, Walk Free GSI). Wide uncertainty; the closest thing to true scale.
Detected
People officially identified by authorities in a year (UNODC). A floor — only those found and recorded.
Reported
Contacts to a hotline (Polaris/NHTH). Reflects awareness and willingness to call — not prevalence, and a significant undercount.

Recognize & respond

Spot the signs — for everyone, and for frontline roles

Framework

These are recognized indicator frameworks from the ILO, DHS, Polaris, and sector experts. They describe situations that warrant a closer look — never a way to judge a person. Trafficking can affect anyone of any gender, age, nationality, or background.

Before you read a single list, read this. No indicator is proof. A person’s race, ethnicity, sex, or religion is never an indicator. Do not investigate, confront, or “rescue.” If you have a concern, report it — call 911 in an emergency, or the National Human Trafficking Hotline at 1‑888‑373‑7888 — and let trained specialists take it from there. As Polaris puts it: it’s less about memorizing signs than knowing the story — “context and proximity.”
● ILO indicators of forced labour (11) ILO 2025▸
Forced labour is “all work or service which is exacted from any person under the threat of a penalty and for which the person has not offered themself voluntarily” (ILO Convention No. 29). The presence of a single indicator does not confirm forced labour, but it signals risk and warrants further inquiry.
  • Abuse of vulnerability
  • Deception
  • Restriction of movement
  • Isolation
  • Physical and sexual violence
  • Intimidation and threats
  • Retention of identity documents
  • Withholding of wages
  • Debt bondage
  • Abusive working and living conditions
  • Excessive overtime
● DHS Blue Campaign — general signs ▸
  • Disconnected from family, friends, or support networks
  • Defers to, or is controlled by, a companion who speaks for them
  • Appears coached on what to say
  • Has their freedom of movement restricted
  • Lives or works under unreasonable security measures
DHS: the presence or absence of any indicator is not proof; report to ICE HSI 1‑866‑347‑2423 or the National Hotline.
● Sector‑specific — healthcare & aviation ▸
Healthcare (HEAL Trafficking). Encounters are a key chance to help. The PEARR approach: Privacy, Educate, Ask, Respect, Respond — a survivor‑centered, trauma‑informed way to raise concerns without endangering the patient.
Aviation (Blue Lightning Initiative, DOT + CBP). Watch for: a controlled or submissive companion; someone who seems held against their will; third‑party control or a “quota”; branding/ownership tattoos; an unaccompanied minor traveling under unusual circumstances.

The one thing to remember

If something feels wrong, you don’t need to be certain, and you should not act alone. Save the number — 1‑888‑373‑7888, text 233733 — and make the call. Trained advocates take it from there, 24/7, confidentially.

How to read the numbers honestly

Estimate — modeled, wide uncertaintyDetected — officially identifiedReported — hotline contacts, undercountCase data — de‑identified recordsFramework — official guidance
  • Estimates ≠ detections ≠ hotline reports. They measure different things and should never be summed or directly compared.
  • Hotline data reflect awareness and willingness to reach out, not how common trafficking is.
  • Some widely‑circulated trafficking statistics are unverifiable; figures here were limited to those confirmable in a primary source. Where a commonly‑cited number couldn’t be verified, it was left out.
  • This page does not profile or score individuals and collects no data. It exists to connect people to help.
