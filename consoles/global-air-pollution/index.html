How to read this. Every figure is a World Bank reported value for a specific year — always shown next to the number, because the latest available year varies (PM2.5 exposure is modeled through 2020, the air-pollution death rate through 2019, clean-cooking access through 2023). This is descriptive: it shows where exposure and its health burden stand and how they have moved since 2000. It does not predict the future, and cross-country comparisons carry differences in measurement and completeness.

Data & method. Source: World Bank Open Data — World Development Indicators, pulled live from the public Indicators API v2 (api.worldbank.org/v2) when reachable, otherwise from an embedded snapshot whose vintage is shown above. Indicators: mean PM2.5 exposure EN.ATM.PM25.MC.M3; population exposed above the WHO interim guideline (>10 µg/m³) EN.ATM.PM25.MC.ZS; mortality attributable to ambient and household air pollution SH.STA.AIRP.P5; access to clean fuels and technologies for cooking EG.CFT.ACCS.ZS. The World Bank compiles these from the WHO (Global Health Observatory and the Global Burden of Disease collaboration), satellite-derived PM2.5 surfaces (van Donkelaar et al.) and national statistics.
Sources of exposure. The air-pollution death rate combines two sources the World Bank reports together: ambient (outdoor) PM2.5 — traffic, industry, power generation, dust and biomass burning — and household (indoor) air pollution from burning solid fuels (wood, dung, charcoal, coal) for cooking and heating. The clean-cooking indicator is shown here as a proxy for the household source: where access is low, indoor smoke is a large share of the burden.
Honesty. All numbers are measured World Bank reported values — but the latest available year differs by country and indicator, so it is always displayed; PM2.5 and the death rate are themselves modeled estimates (satellite-plus-ground exposure surfaces and burden-of-disease attribution), not direct counts. The WHO guideline framing and the choice of which ~55 countries and 4 indicators to show is interpretive. The EN.ATM.PM25.MC.ZS series counts the population above the WHO interim target of 10 µg/m³; the current (2021) WHO annual guideline is the stricter 5 µg/m³, so even more people live above that level. This tool is descriptive, not predictive; it does not forecast. No personal or protected health information is used — only public country-level aggregates.
