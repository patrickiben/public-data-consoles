How to read this. Every figure is a World Bank reported value for a specific year — always shown next to the number. The headline rate is deaths per 100,000 people, so it already adjusts for population size; it is not a count and not adjusted for how much each country drives. This is descriptive: it shows where road-death rates stand and how they have moved since 2000. It does not predict the future, and cross-country comparisons carry differences in measurement and reporting completeness.

◑ The income paradox — by income group & region measuredinterpretive
The paradox. Lower-income countries own far fewer vehicles per person, yet carry far higher road-death rates. The WHO Global Status Report attributes this gap to factors such as vulnerable road users (pedestrians, cyclists, motorcyclists), mixed traffic, vehicle and road conditions, and post-crash emergency care — interpretive framing, not a World Bank ranking.

Data & method. Source: World Bank Open Data — World Development Indicators, pulled live from the public Indicators API v2 (api.worldbank.org/v2) when reachable, otherwise from an embedded snapshot whose vintage is shown above. Indicators: road-traffic mortality SH.STA.TRAF.P5 (deaths per 100,000 people); deaths from injury SH.DTH.INJR.ZS (% of total deaths). The World Bank compiles the road-traffic series from the WHO Global Status Report on Road Safety modelled comparable estimates; the injury-share series from WHO Global Health Estimates.
Interpretive context. The figures of ~1.19 million road deaths per year and road injury being a leading cause of death for people aged 5–29 are from the interpretive WHO Global Status Report on Road Safety 2023; they are not World Bank API values and are shown as context, not derived here.
Honesty. All series values are measured World Bank reported values — the latest available year is displayed next to each (the road-traffic series is currently 2019 worldwide); missing values are shown as "—" rather than guessed or set to zero. Aggregates are the Bank's population-weighted estimates. The choice of which ~55 countries to show, and the "income paradox" framing, is interpretive. This tool is descriptive, not predictive; it does not forecast and is non-judgmental about why rates differ. No personal or protected health information is used — only public country-level aggregates.
